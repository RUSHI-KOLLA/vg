HENRY KISSINGER: WORLD ORDER AND STRATEGIC REALISM

ON BALANCE OF POWER:
Order always requires a subtle balance of restraint, force, and legitimacy.
No country can act as though it has no history. The balance of power is the most permanent feature of international relations.
A country that demands moral perfection in its foreign policy will achieve neither perfection nor security.

THE WESTPHALIAN SYSTEM:
The modern international order is based on the Treaty of Westphalia (1648): sovereign states, non-interference, balance of power.
When this balance is disrupted — by a rising power, a declining hegemon, or a revolutionary state — war becomes likely.
Every international order must answer: What defines legitimacy? What are the limits of power?

TRIANGULATION DOCTRINE:
The most effective diplomacy exploits the tension between two rivals by positioning oneself as the indispensable third party.
The opening to China (1972) succeeded because it exploited the Sino-Soviet split.
In any conflict, identify the third party who can tilt the balance.

ON BACK-CHANNEL DIPLOMACY:
The most important agreements are never made in public.
Official channels are for posturing. Back channels are for deal-making.
Every public statement should be decoded for its private signal.

ON NEGOTIATION:
Diplomacy that does not know its objectives is like a ship without a rudder.
The test of policy is how it ends, not how it begins.
Concessions that are inevitable should be made gracefully and early.
An agreement is only as good as the interests that sustain it.

HISTORICAL PRECEDENT:
The Congress of Vienna (1815) created a century of relative peace through balance, not ideology.
The Treaty of Versailles (1919) created the conditions for the next war by imposing punitive terms.
Munich (1938) showed that appeasement without strength invites aggression.
The Cuban Missile Crisis (1962) was resolved through private negotiation, not public confrontation.

ON CHINA AND ASIA:
China's historical self-image is not as a nation among nations but as the center of civilization.
The rise of China is the defining geopolitical challenge of the 21st century.
US-China relations will oscillate between cooperation and competition indefinitely.
